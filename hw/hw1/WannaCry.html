WannaCry (2017)

WannaCry was a large-scale ransomware attack that took place in May 2017, infecting over 230,000 computers across more than 150 countries. The ransomware exploited a vulnerability in Microsoft Windows called EternalBlue, originally developed by the NSA and later leaked by the hacking group known as the Shadow Brokers. WannaCry spread rapidly across networks, encrypting files and demanding ransom payments in Bitcoin to unlock the infected systems. Prominent victims of the attack included organizations like Britain’s National Health Service (NHS), which suffered significant disruptions, including canceled surgeries and appointments.

The ransomware leveraged the EternalBlue exploit to propagate autonomously between systems, making it particularly dangerous. Although Microsoft had issued a patch for the vulnerability two months before the attack, many organizations had not yet updated their systems, leaving them vulnerable. Financial damages from the attack were estimated to be between $4 billion and $8 billion globally due to operational downtime, lost productivity, and costs related to recovery and ransom payments.

A critical turning point in stopping WannaCry’s spread came when a security researcher, Marcus Hutchins, discovered a “kill switch” embedded in the malware's code. By registering a specific domain name the malware was querying, Hutchins inadvertently halted the infection’s rapid spread. However, systems that had already been infected remained locked, and paying the ransom often did not result in files being decrypted as promised by the attackers.

The WannaCry attack underscored the importance of regularly updating software and patching known security vulnerabilities. It also highlighted the risks associated with using outdated or unsupported operating systems, as many victims were running older versions of Windows. Despite the attack being contained, the EternalBlue exploit remains a significant tool in other cyberattacks, showcasing the long-lasting effects of the WannaCry incident.
