MyDoom (2004)

MyDoom, first identified in January 2004, is one of the most destructive and fast-spreading email worms in history. The virus primarily spread through infected email attachments that appeared as bounced messages or seemingly legitimate files. When users clicked on the attachment, MyDoom would infect the system, automatically harvesting email addresses from the user's contacts and sending itself to those addresses. This self-replication mechanism allowed it to spread rapidly, causing a massive global outbreak.

Once MyDoom infiltrated a system, it created a backdoor that allowed hackers to gain remote access to the infected computer, enabling them to control the machine or use it as part of larger cyberattacks. One of the most notorious aspects of MyDoom was its ability to launch Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks. These attacks overwhelmed major websites with traffic, causing significant disruptions. Google, Yahoo, and other large websites were temporarily slowed or brought down due to the flood of traffic caused by infected machines.

The financial damage caused by MyDoom is estimated to be over $38 billion, making it one of the costliest viruses in history. It not only disrupted personal computers but also crippled businesses and internet infrastructure globally. The virus's ability to spread quickly, combined with its dual functionality as a tool for email harvesting and DDoS attacks, solidified its place in cybersecurity history as one of the most potent malware outbreaks.
